🌿 PanchkarmaSetu — Project Management Analysis

Project Overview: PanchkarmaSetu is a Django-based Ayurvedic healthcare web application that digitizes the end-to-end Panchkarma treatment workflow. It supports three user roles — Patient, Therapist, and Centre Head — and manages appointment booking, dosha-based diagnosis, treatment cycle tracking, attendance/vitals recording, PDF report generation, and feedback collection.

---

1. Life Cycle Model: Hybrid (Agile + Waterfall)

Identified Model: Hybrid

Logical Justification

PanchkarmaSetu does not fit neatly into a pure Waterfall or pure Agile model. The evidence from the project structure points clearly to a Hybrid approach:

Phase | Model Used | Evidence from Project
--------------------------------------------------
Requirements & Architecture | Waterfall | Role hierarchy (Patient → Therapist → Centre Head), data models (Appointment, DiagnosisReport, TreatmentCycle, Attendance) were firmly defined upfront
Database Schema Design | Waterfall | A stable, relational schema was designed first — changing Appointment after building DiagnosisReport (OneToOneField) would be costly
UI Development | Agile/Iterative | 12 templates were built and refined iteratively; the Bootstrap 5 refactoring happened as a later iteration
Feature Delivery | Agile | Features were delivered in functional slices: Auth → Booking → Diagnosis → Treatment → Attendance → PDF → Feedback
PDF Report Generation (pdf_utils.py, 19 KB) | Incremental | This specialized utility was added in a later sprint after the core workflow was stable

Why NOT Pure Waterfall?
- The UI went through at least one major refactoring cycle (inline CSS → Bootstrap 5). Pure Waterfall does not accommodate this kind of late-phase UI revision.
- New features (e.g., Notification, Attendance vitals like avg_bp, pulse_bpm) were inserted into the existing schema mid-development, suggesting sprint-based incremental additions.

Why NOT Pure Agile?
- The core database schema (models.py) follows a top-down, pre-planned relational design — not something typically discovered sprint-by-sprint.
- The three-role access control system (patient, therapist, centre_head) required upfront architectural definition before any code could be written.

Conclusion
Hybrid model — Waterfall for architecture/database design, Agile/Incremental for UI, feature delivery, and refinements. This is the pragmatic choice for a small-team healthcare management system where the domain (Panchkarma treatment flow) is well-understood but implementation details evolve.

2. Team & Scenario: Roles and Sudden Departure Impact

Defined Team Roles

# | Role | Responsibilities in PanchkarmaSetu
--------------------------------------------------
1 | Project Manager / Lead Developer | Overall coordination, milestone tracking, final code review, deployment decisions
2 | Backend Developer | Django views (views.py, 24 KB), URL routing, business logic for all 3 user flows, access control
3 | Database Designer / DBA | models.py schema, migrations, query optimization, SQLite to production DB migration planning
4 | Frontend Developer | All 12 HTML templates, Bootstrap 5 integration, glassmorphism design, responsive layouts
5 | QA / Tester | Functional testing across all 3 roles, form validation, edge cases (slot conflicts, cancelled cycles)
6 | Domain Expert / Business Analyst | Panchkarma treatment knowledge — dosha types, treatment duration, clinical vitals definitions, PDF report content

---

Impact/Risk if a Member Leaves Suddenly

HIGH RISK — Backend Developer Leaves
- Immediate Impact: All core business logic halts. views.py contains 24 KB of complex, interconnected logic handling booking, diagnosis, treatment cycles, attendance, and PDF generation.
- Risk: No other team member can quickly understand the role-based access patterns, slot-availability logic, or treatment state machine.
- Mitigation: Maintain thorough code comments and a handover document; pair-program critical modules. The new developer would need ~2 weeks minimum to ramp up.

HIGH RISK — Domain Expert / Business Analyst Leaves
- Immediate Impact: The team loses understanding of what is clinically correct. Errors in dosha assignment (vata, pitta, kapha, combinations), treatment duration, or vitals interpretation could go undetected.
- Risk: The PDF reports (pdf_utils.py) and DiagnosisReport logic could produce medically incorrect outputs — a critical risk in a healthcare application.
- Mitigation: Document all domain rules in a business requirements specification before this person exits. Their knowledge is irreplaceable technically.

MEDIUM RISK — Frontend Developer Leaves
- Immediate Impact: New feature UIs and template modifications stall. However, existing 12 templates continue functioning.
- Risk: Bootstrap 5 customizations and the glassmorphism design system would be hard to extend consistently without the original developer.
- Mitigation: Establish a design system guide and component documentation. The backend developer could take on minor frontend fixes.

MEDIUM RISK — Database Designer Leaves
- Immediate Impact: New migrations and schema changes become risky. Existing db.sqlite3 and migrations continue working.
- Risk: Any future schema change (e.g., adding insurance fields, multi-centre support) could cause data corruption if handled by someone unfamiliar with Django's migration system.
- Mitigation: Document the ERD and migration strategy. Keep migrations clean and well-commented.

LOW RISK — QA/Tester Leaves
- Immediate Impact: Structured testing halts. Informal testing by developers continues.
- Risk: Edge cases (e.g., double-booking a time slot, cancelling midway through a cycle) may slip through to production.
- Mitigation: Write automated tests in tests.py (currently minimal at 60 bytes) to reduce dependency on manual QA.

LOW RISK — Project Manager Leaves
- Immediate Impact: Coordination and milestone tracking become informal.
- Risk: Scope creep and missed deadlines, but technical work continues.
- Mitigation: A senior developer can temporarily absorb lightweight PM duties in a small team.

3. Risk Management: Three Prioritized Project Risks

Risks are rated on Likelihood (L) × Impact (I) = Risk Score (1–5 scale each)

---

Risk 1 — TECHNICAL: Single-Point-of-Failure in Business Logic

Category | Detail
-------------------------
Category | Technical Risk
Description | The entire application's business logic is concentrated in a single views.py file (24,711 bytes). All three user workflows — patient booking, therapist diagnosis, centre head management — are tightly coupled here.
Likelihood | 4/5 — Common in small Django projects built under time pressure
Impact | 5/5 — A bug or corruption here can break all user flows simultaneously
Risk Score | 20/25 — CRITICAL
Example Scenario | A logic error in the treatment state machine allows a patient to book a new appointment while an active treatment cycle exists, causing data inconsistency in TreatmentCycle records
Mitigation | Refactor into service layer classes; add unit tests in tests.py; implement Django signals for state transitions

---

Risk 2 — SCHEDULE: Scope Creep from Healthcare Feature Additions

Category | Detail
-------------------------
Category | Schedule Risk
Description | The domain (Ayurvedic healthcare) has a high potential for feature expansion requests — multi-centre support, insurance billing, teleconsultation, herb inventory management, etc. — any of which could push deadlines significantly.
Likelihood | 4/5 — Stakeholders in healthcare projects consistently request additions once the core is working
Impact | 4/5 — Each major feature addition (e.g., multi-centre) would require schema changes, view rewrites, and template updates
Risk Score | 16/25 — HIGH
Example Scenario | After seeing the working appointment booking flow, the centre head requests that PanchkarmaSetu also track herb/oil inventory per treatment cycle — a new model, new views, new templates, and domain knowledge requirements
Mitigation | Maintain a formal change request process; freeze scope at least 3 weeks before submission; use a product backlog to defer non-critical features to v2.0

---

Risk 3 — RESOURCE: SQLite Database in Production

Category | Detail
-------------------------
Category | Resource / Technical Risk
Description | The project currently uses SQLite (db.sqlite3, 208 KB). SQLite is not suitable for concurrent multi-user access in a real clinic deployment — it has write-lock limitations and no built-in connection pooling.
Likelihood | 3/5 — Will definitely be a problem if deployed to a real clinic with simultaneous bookings
Impact | 4/5 — Data corruption or race conditions in concurrent appointment booking and attendance marking
Risk Score | 12/25 — MEDIUM-HIGH
Example Scenario | Two patients simultaneously submit a booking for the same time slot; SQLite's write lock causes one request to fail without proper error handling, or both records are saved creating a double-booking conflict
Mitigation | Migrate to PostgreSQL before any real-world deployment; use Django's select_for_update() for slot-booking transactions; already partially mitigated by unique_together on Attendance model

4. Leadership Styles: Directive vs. Collaborative

---

Directive Leadership Style

Definition: The leader makes decisions unilaterally, gives clear instructions, and expects them to be followed with minimal discussion. Best used in crisis, time-pressure, or when the team lacks expertise.

Realistic Situation in PanchkarmaSetu:

Scenario — Security Vulnerability Discovered 48 Hours Before Submission

During final testing, the QA tester reports that the book_appointment view in views.py does not properly validate that the logged-in user is a patient — meaning a therapist account can craft a manual URL (/patient/book/) and submit a fake appointment for any patient.

Directive Response:
"This is a critical security flaw. I am making the call right now: we stop all other work. Backend developer — add @login_required and a role check decorator to all patient-facing views before end of day. I'll handle the testing myself. No new features until this is fixed. Here is the exact code pattern to follow — apply it uniformly across all 7 patient-route views."

Why Directive Here?
- Time pressure (48 hours to submission)
- Clear right answer exists — role-based access control is well-understood
- Risk of divergent approaches if everyone discusses it
- No time for consensus-building

---

Collaborative Leadership Style

Definition: The leader facilitates discussion, encourages team input, and reaches decisions collectively. Best used for complex design problems, long-term planning, or when multiple specialists hold relevant knowledge.

Realistic Situation in PanchkarmaSetu:

Scenario — Designing the Dosha Diagnosis Workflow

Early in the project, the team debates how the diagnosis report should work. The backend developer wants a simple text field in DiagnosisReport. The domain expert insists on a multi-step questionnaire (Prakriti assessment). The frontend developer is concerned about a complex multi-page form. The centre head (real-world stakeholder) wants reports to be downloadable immediately.

Collaborative Response:
"Let's sit together for an hour. Backend team — walk us through what's technically feasible in our timeline. Domain expert — explain the minimum clinically valid dosha assessment needed. Frontend — show us two mockups: a simple form and a wizard-style flow. Centre head — what do your therapists actually need in the PDF? Let's agree on an MVP definition before anyone writes code."

Result: The team agrees on the current DiagnosisReport model — a therapist fills in dosha + diagnosis_result + recommended_treatment — simple enough to build quickly but clinically meaningful enough to satisfy the domain expert. The pdf_utils.py PDF report was added as a later enhancement based on this collaborative grounding.

Why Collaborative Here?
- No single person has all the answers — the domain expert, developer, and stakeholder each hold critical knowledge
- Early-stage design — mistakes here propagate through the entire codebase
- Team buy-in matters — the frontend developer needs to feel ownership over the UX choice
- Complex trade-offs between clinical correctness, technical simplicity, and UX — not solvable by directive command
